FMCG Demand Forecast

A production AI system that tells an Indonesian distributor how much of 1,300+ products to stock — 30 days ahead, every week, automatically

An Indonesian FMCG distributor runs four "dark stores" — small warehouses that deliver everyday goods (instant noodles, bottled water, cigarettes, rice, syrup) to thousands of small shops. Every week, buyers must decide how much of each product to purchase. Order too little and shelves go empty; order too much and capital is frozen in slow stock. This project replaces gut-feel ordering with a deep-learning forecasting pipeline that runs in production: every Sunday night it reads eight years of live sales data, retrains itself on a GPU, and writes a 30-day demand forecast for every product in every warehouse back into the company database.

Why a range instead of one number? Look at the rice (Beras Raja Lele): shops buy it in unpredictable bulk — quiet for days, then a spike. A single-number forecast is almost guaranteed wrong on any given day. Instead the model outputs a low estimate (P10) and a high estimate (P90). A planner stocks near the high end for must-never-run-out products and near the middle for expensive slow movers. The range is the product — it converts a guess into a decision tool for safety stock.

Why NHITS — and why not the alternatives?

  • Why not classic statistics (ARIMA, Prophet, exponential smoothing)? They fit one model per product, in isolation. With 4,000+ series — many noisy or short — each tiny model overfits its own noise and none can use signals like "Ramadan starts in 20 days" or "it rained 3 days straight." A single global neural model learns shared patterns across all products at once: what payday does to noodles also teaches it what payday does to coffee.
  • Why not a plain LSTM? That was the first production version (LSTM encoder + GRU decoder). It worked, but tended toward flat multi-day forecasts and was slow to train per series. It was superseded, not abandoned blindly — the comparison was measured.
  • Why not the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT)? I migrated to TFT first — it's the textbook choice. In practice its data-loading machinery, not the GPU, was the bottleneck: ~130 seconds per epoch with the RTX 5090 sitting nearly idle. NHITS (a hierarchical-interpolation network from Nixtla's neuralforecast) trains the entire 1,320-series tier in ~45 seconds total with equal-or-better accuracy, while still accepting the same calendar, weather, and out-of-stock signals. Speed matters here: a fast model makes weekly retraining and honest A/B experiments affordable.
  • Why keep LightGBM and a weighted average at all? Neural networks need history. For young products, gradient boosting on lag features is more reliable; for brand-new ones, a day-of-week weighted average beats any model pretending to know more than it does.

The honest reading: ~85% of predictive power comes from recent sales momentum (rolling averages over the last 1–4 weeks). Weather contributes a real but modest ~6% (rain keeps shop owners from restocking trips), and calendar timing ~5%. The big cultural events — Ramadan, Lebaran — score low here because this tier only sees young products with under a year of history, often not even one full Ramadan. The neural tier, trained on up to two years per series plus a continuous "days until Lebaran" ramp, is where seasonal signals do their work — visible in the Syrup Kartika chart above, a product that effectively exists for Lebaran gift baskets.

The trap most forecasts fall into: empty shelves look like zero demand

If a product is out of stock for a week, the sales ledger records zero — but demand wasn't zero, it was invisible. A model trained naively on that data learns "this product sometimes sells nothing for a week" and forecasts low, which causes under-ordering, which causes more stock-outs: a self-fulfilling death spiral for the product.

This pipeline cross-references the stock ledger (1.2M+ records) against sales: days where end-of-day stock was zero are flagged on 70,559 product-days (2.3% of all data). The neural tier receives the flag as a "don't trust this day" signal, the boosting tier drops those days as training targets, and the averaging tier excludes them from its statistics. Censored data is a statistics problem; pretending it isn't one is how forecasting systems quietly fail.

Encoding how Indonesia actually shops

  • 18 Indonesian calendar features — Ramadan month, Lebaran peak week, a continuous 60-day "Lebaran approaching" ramp, THR (holiday bonus) payout week, payday window (25th–5th), Chinese New Year, Idul Adha, back-to-school, rainy season (Oct–Apr), long weekends. Islamic dates are computed automatically from the Hijri calendar — zero annual maintenance.
  • Closed-day redistribution — warehouses close Sundays and holidays, but shop demand doesn't pause; it lands on the preceding days. Closed-day rows are removed and their demand is pushed 60/40 onto the two prior working days, mirroring how orders actually arrive: 
       Fri        Sat        SUN (closed)
  ┌────┐    ┌────┐         ┌╌╌╌╌┐
  │    │+40%│    │+60%  ◀──┊ X  ┊   Sunday's demand doesn't vanish —
  └────┘    └────┘         └╌╌╌╌┘   it shifts to Friday & Saturday
  • Live weather — daily rainfall and temperature per warehouse location (historical + 16-day forecast from Open-Meteo), because a rainy week measurably suppresses restocking trips.

How good is it? Metrics, in plain language

All numbers below come from rolling-origin backtesting on the live production data: the model is repeatedly trained on history up to a cutoff, asked to predict the next 30 days blind, and graded — three separate test months, results averaged. This is the strictest honest test short of waiting a month in real time.

MetricResultWhat it means
WAPE 46.0%volume-weighted errorFor every 100 units that sold, the daily per-product forecast was off by ~46 units on average. Sounds high — but this is daily, per-product, per-warehouse granularity, the hardest possible cut. Over a full month per product (what a buyer actually orders against), the aggregate error drops to ~33%.
Bias −13%systematic tiltThe model under-forecasts by 13% overall — down from −29% raw, halved by a calibration layer (below). Bias is the metric a buyer feels: it's the difference between consistently empty and consistently overstocked shelves.
PI80 = 74.8%band honestyThe "80% confidence band" actually contained the true value 74.8% of the time. A band that claims 80% and delivers ~75% is slightly overconfident but usable; many systems claim 80% and deliver 50%.
Skill > 0beats the baselineBetter than the naive strategy ("next month = recent weekly pattern"). Naive baselines are embarrassingly strong in FMCG; any system that doesn't report this comparison is hiding something.

Why these metrics and not the usual suspects? MAPE — the most commonly quoted forecasting metric — divides by actual sales, so slow products with near-zero sales explode the average and dominate the score; it rewards models for being accurate on products nobody buys. RMSE squares errors, so one bestseller spike outweighs a hundred meaningful misses, and it says nothing about direction. WAPE weights error by sales volume (being right on Indomie matters more than on candles), and Bias captures the systematic tilt that actually drives inventory cost. For the uncertainty bands, coverage checks honesty and CRPS (a proper scoring rule) checks sharpness — both improved in the shipped configuration.

The calibration layer: fixing bias without lying to yourself

The raw neural model consistently under-forecast (~−29%) — largely because demand grew ~46% over two years and a model anchored on history lags a growth trend. The obvious fix, "multiply forecasts up by the recent error," is dangerous: measured on a single month, that correction factor is noise. The first version (v4) did exactly that and failed its ship gate — the factor even flipped sign between test windows. The version that shipped (v8) pools the estimate across multiple backtest windows, shrinks it toward 1.0 in proportion to evidence, and is only allowed to scale up, never down, capped at +30%. Deliberately under-correcting beats overconfidently overshooting. Result: bias halved, every other metric improved or held.

Nine versions, two rejections, one shipped

This is the part of ML work that rarely makes it into portfolios: most experiments fail, and the discipline is in measuring that. Every change had to pass a pre-declared ship gate ("bias must move toward zero without accuracy or band quality regressing") on the rolling backtest before touching production:

v1–2

Is the model undertrained? Quadrupled the training budget — bias barely moved. Training time was not the lever. Also exposed that a single test month gives wildly unstable bias estimates.

v3

Rolling-origin backtesting introduced. Three test months instead of one. Bias estimate stabilized at −26±4% — now trustworthy enough to act on.

v4

Bias correction, take one — REJECTED. Correction factor estimated from one validation month flipped sign between windows (scaled up 1.3× in one, down 0.8× in another). Accuracy regressed. Gate failed; feature stayed off in production.

v5

Clean baseline locked. Re-ran with correction off; metrics reproduced bit-for-bit (seeded). Also first run with out-of-stock flags live: 70,559 censored days detected.

v6

Scaler A/B — REJECTED. Standard scaling worsened bias. Same run caught a config knob that silently did nothing — found because the registry logs effective config, not intended config.

v7

Shorter training window A/B. 365 days vs 730: accuracy flat, variance 3× tighter. Kept for stability; disproved the favored "growth-anchoring" hypothesis as the main bias driver.

v8

Bias correction, take two — SHIPPED. Pooled multi-window estimate + shrinkage toward 1.0 + asymmetric clip (only scale up, max +30%). Gate passed: bias −29% → −13%, accuracy and band quality improved simultaneously.

v9

Production verification. Reproduced v8 exactly; corrected metrics now persisted to the run registry. The configuration running every Sunday since.

Beyond demand

The demand forecast is half the system: a companion sales pipeline (per-region LSTM with multi-head attention) forecasts revenue and cost-of-goods, feeding a purchase-recommendation engine. A sanitized, fully-runnable version of the architecture — live databases replaced by a synthetic Indonesian-FMCG data generator — is open-sourced on GitHub.
